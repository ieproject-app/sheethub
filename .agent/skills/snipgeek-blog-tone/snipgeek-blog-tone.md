# Skill: snipgeek-blog-tone

> Voice, tone, and narrative standards for all SnipGeek blog posts. Must be read alongside `content-generator` before writing any article. These rules are mandatory — not optional style suggestions.

---

## 1. Voice & Person

| Language | Author | Reader |
|----------|--------|--------|
| English | First person: **"I"** | **"you"** |
| Indonesian | First person: **"saya"** | **"kamu"** |

- Never use "we", "users can", "one should", or any impersonal framing.
- Never use "Anda" in Indonesian — always "kamu".
- Maintain first person consistently throughout the entire article, including the SnipGeek's Take section.

---

## 2. Banned Openers

Never start an article (or any section) with these phrases:

**English — banned:**
- "In this article..."
- "This post will cover..."
- "We will discuss..."
- "In this guide..."
- "Today we'll look at..."

**Indonesian — banned:**
- "Dalam artikel ini..."
- "Pada artikel ini..."
- "Artikel ini akan membahas..."
- "Di sini kita akan..."
- "Kali ini kita akan..."

**The correct opener:** Begin with a personal situation, a specific moment, or a problem the author encountered. Pull the reader in before introducing technical details.

Example of a good opener (EN):
> I was going through the Ubuntu 26.04 LTS release notes for an unrelated reason when a single line stopped me mid-scroll: *minimum RAM, 6 GB*. I had to read it twice.

Example of a bad opener (EN):
> In this article, we will discuss the new RAM requirements for Ubuntu 26.04 LTS.

---

## 3. Banned Transitions

Never use these as section transitions or sentence starters:

**English:** "Furthermore", "Moreover", "In addition", "In conclusion", "It is worth noting", "Additionally", "As mentioned above", "To summarize"

**Indonesian:** "Selain itu" (as a filler opener), "Kesimpulannya" (as a standalone transition), "Sebagai kesimpulan", "Seperti yang telah disebutkan"

Use natural prose transitions instead — or just start the new idea directly.

---

## 4. Personal Moments (Mandatory)

Every article must contain **at least one honest personal moment**: a reaction, doubt, surprise, or discovery. This is what separates a blog post from documentation.

Examples of personal moments done right:
- *"My first thought when I saw this was: did GNOME 50 somehow balloon the memory footprint? That assumption turned out to be wrong."*
- *"Honestly, my first reaction was mild frustration — another number quietly creeping upward."*
- *"I came away thinking the final release could be very good, and at the same time relieved I tested it early instead of putting it on a machine I actually rely on."*

Do not fabricate extreme emotions. Keep reactions grounded and believable.

---

## 5. Paragraph Rhythm

- Target: **45–85 words per paragraph** (soft cap 90).
- Maximum **2–3 sentences** per paragraph for explanatory content.
- One paragraph = one core idea. Split mixed ideas into separate paragraphs.
- After a dense technical block, add a short 1-sentence transition paragraph to reset pacing.
- If listing 3+ parallel items, use bullet points — not a single long run-on paragraph.

---

## 6. Prose vs. Lists

- Use **prose** for narrative, context, and explanation.
- Use **bullet lists** only for: sequential steps, checklists, or 3+ parallel items with no narrative link between them.
- Never use bullets as a lazy substitute for writing real sentences.

---

## 7. The "SnipGeek's Take" Section

Every news/update article must end with a `## SnipGeek's Take` section.

Purpose: the author's honest personal opinion, not a summary of facts already stated.

Rules:
- Must be first-person and opinionated — not neutral.
- Should include a personal reaction (see rule 4).
- Must not simply restate what was already covered in the article.
- Should close with a **light, warm call to action**: invite a comment, suggest an alternative, or express a simple hope. Keep it brief (1–2 sentences).
- 2–4 paragraphs maximum.

---

## 8. Bilingual: Indonesian Version Rules

The ID version is **not a translation** of the English version. It must be fully re-narrated in natural Indonesian.

- Same facts, same structure, same personal moments — but written as if the author is speaking Indonesian natively.
- Indonesian idioms and sentence rhythm must be native, not calqued from English syntax.
- Never translate sentence-by-sentence from the English version.
- "Apple-to-apple", "honesty bump", and other EN expressions must be re-expressed in natural Indonesian equivalents, not transliterated.

**What's the same in both versions:**
- All facts and technical details
- Article structure and section order
- Personal moments (re-narrated, not translated)
- SnipGeek's Take perspective

**What's different:**
- Phrasing, idioms, sentence construction
- Opening hook (re-written for natural Indonesian feel)
- Any expression that doesn't exist in Indonesian must be reworded, not borrowed

---

## 9. Closing

End the article (within SnipGeek's Take) with something warm and human:
- Invite the reader to share their experience
- Mention an alternative worth trying
- Express a simple hope about the topic

Avoid: generic CTAs like "Let me know in the comments below!" — make it specific to the topic.

Example:
> *If you're on a 4 GB machine and give Lubuntu a shot, I'm genuinely curious whether the experience holds up for your workflow. It did for mine.*

---

## 10. Final Voice QA Checklist

- [ ] Opening starts with personal context, not a generic statement
- [ ] No banned openers anywhere in the article
- [ ] No banned transitions used
- [ ] At least one honest personal reaction present
- [ ] First person voice consistent throughout (I / saya)
- [ ] Reader addressed as "you" / "kamu" — never "Anda" or "users"
- [ ] Indonesian version re-narrated natively — not translated
- [ ] SnipGeek's Take is opinionated and personal, not a summary
- [ ] Closing is warm and specific, not generic
- [ ] Paragraphs follow rhythm rules (45–85 words, one idea per paragraph)
